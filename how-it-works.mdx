ProofBridge replaces the trusted intermediaries found in traditional bridges with a direct peer-to-peer match between two parties: a **Maker** who provides liquidity and a **Bridger** who wants to transfer assets. The trade settles trustlessly because neither party can receive funds unless a zero-knowledge proof confirms both sides of the transaction are valid.

## The two roles

### Makers

Makers are liquidity providers. You deposit tokens on one chain and publish a **liquidity ad** listing the route, amount, and rate you offer. Your funds stay locked in the **AdManager** contract — they are not accessible to anyone else until a valid ZK proof confirms a completed trade.

Becoming a Maker is how you earn fees on ProofBridge. Every time a Bridger uses your liquidity to settle a transfer, you receive their deposited tokens on the source chain.

### Bridgers

Bridgers are users who want to move assets from one chain to another. You browse the marketplace of liquidity ads, select one that matches your route and amount, then deposit tokens on the **OrderPortal** contract on the source chain. Once the deposit is confirmed and the proof is verified, your destination-chain tokens are released automatically.

## End-to-end bridging flow

<Steps>
  <Step title="Maker posts a liquidity ad">
    The Maker creates an ad on the **AdManager** contract on their chosen chain, locking the destination-chain tokens they want to provide. The ad specifies the token pair, the available amount, and the exchange rate. It appears in the ProofBridge marketplace for Bridgers to discover.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Bridger requests authorization">
    Before depositing, the Bridger requests a pre-authorization from the relayer. This step confirms the ad is still available and that the trade parameters are valid. The relayer returns a signed approval.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Bridger creates an order on-chain">
    With the pre-authorization in hand, the Bridger deposits source-chain tokens into the **OrderPortal** contract. This creates a cryptographically signed order on-chain. The contract records the order hash in a Merkle Mountain Range (MMR) tree — a tamper-proof log of all deposits on that chain.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Maker locks liquidity for the order">
    The Maker is notified of the Bridger's deposit and locks the corresponding amount from their ad against the specific order on the **AdManager** contract. The Maker's chain also records this commitment in its own MMR tree.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Zero-knowledge proof is generated">
    The relayer fetches the Merkle proof (evidence that the deposit exists in the on-chain tree) and passes it to the ZK proof circuit. The circuit produces a cryptographic proof that:

    - The deposit is genuinely recorded in the source chain's MMR tree.
    - The order hash, amounts, and recipients match the agreed terms.
    - The proof cannot be replayed — a unique nullifier is consumed once the proof is used.

    None of the private data (secrets or individual transaction details) is revealed in the proof.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Both chains settle simultaneously">
    The relayer submits the proof to both chains:

    - On the **AdManager** (destination chain): the proof is verified and the Maker's locked tokens are released to the Bridger's designated recipient.
    - On the **OrderPortal** (source chain): the proof is verified and the Bridger's deposited tokens are released to the Maker's designated recipient.

    Both unlocks are independently verifiable. Neither party can receive funds without the proof passing on-chain verification on their respective chain.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  Settlement on both chains is not a single atomic transaction — the relayer submits proofs to each chain separately. However, because both proofs reference the same order hash and nullifier, neither party can receive funds twice or claim funds without completing their side.
</Note>

## What zero-knowledge proofs do for you

You do not need to understand the cryptography to use ProofBridge, but it helps to know what the proofs protect:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Trustless verification" icon="shield-check">
    The on-chain verifier confirms that a deposit really happened and the terms match — without trusting the relayer's word. The proof is the only thing that unlocks funds.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Replay prevention" icon="rotate-left">
    Each proof includes a unique nullifier. Once a proof is used to settle a trade, the same proof cannot be submitted again, preventing double-spending.
  </Card>
  <Card title="No private data exposed" icon="eye-slash">
    The ZK proof confirms that conditions are met without revealing private inputs like secret values used to generate nullifiers.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Tamper-proof order history" icon="database">
    Deposits are recorded in an append-only Merkle Mountain Range tree on each chain. Once recorded, the history cannot be altered — and the proof circuit checks inclusion against this tree.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Security guarantees

ProofBridge's security model layers several protections:

- **EIP-712 order hashes** bind the trade terms to specific chain IDs and contract addresses, preventing cross-chain or cross-contract replay attacks.
- **Bidirectional chain linking** means each contract only accepts proofs from its configured counterpart chain. A proof from an unrecognized chain is rejected.
- **Manager role permissions** on the Merkle tree contracts restrict which contracts can append new order hashes, preventing unauthorized entries.
- **Collateral locking** requires Makers to have funds locked before any order is matched, guaranteeing the Bridger's destination tokens exist before the trade starts.

<Warning>
  ProofBridge is currently in a pre-authorization (stateful relayer) phase. The relayer coordinates proof submission but cannot access or redirect funds — all fund releases require a valid on-chain proof. See the roadmap for the planned transition to a fully stateless, permissionless relayer model.
</Warning>
